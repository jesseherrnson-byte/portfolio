Cantoludus is an educational deck-building card game designed to teach elementary music theory concepts. I co-designed the game while taking a course called Educational Gaming Lab at Wesleyan University. We were tasked with creating any kind of educational game that utilized concepts from the class, so we chose to aim our game at high school/college age students who are in intro level music theory courses.

Cantoludus was designed to be a teaching tool that could be used intermittently throughout the course of a course. It is modular; after students have a lesson on intervals, they can play the base version of the game. Then, once they learn about scales and chords, they can add the chord cards to the deck. Then they can include progressions, then hide chord makeups -- so on and so forth, depending on the class' needs on that given day.

In the game, players start with a simple deck of just one of each note, plus sharps and flats. In the middle is the "Orchestra" where they can "buy" new cards by combining the ones in their hand. They then add the bought cards to their deck, and can play them when drawn. The goal is to gain enough "Impression Points" by collecting and playing cards strategically in order to beat the other player. You can check out the rules or look at the game entry on the Educational Gaming Lab website.

As a little bonus (and inspired by the recent release of Balatro), I ended up making a little digital mockup of the game in Unity. This version was only a prototype, but it demonstrated some additional learning potential –– when cards were played, they would actually make the sound they represent, reinforcing to players what different intervals or chords sounded like. It was also easier to implement the "blind chords" mode in a digital format.
